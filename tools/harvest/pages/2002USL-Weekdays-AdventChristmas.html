The Season of Advent:

Advent is not usually a full four weeks, but between three and four weeks long, depending on what weekday Christmas is in a particular year. Advent begins on the Sunday between Nov. 27 and Dec. 3.
Thus, the readings listed here for the "Third Week of Advent" might not all be used, since the last eight days before Christmas use the readings assigned for Dec. 17-24, as listed at the bottom of this table.

Exceptions for 2026:

  • Dec. 8 is the Solemnity of the Immaculate Conception of the Blessed Virgin Mary, with its own Proper Readings.
  • Dec. 12 is the Feast of Our Lady of Guadalupe, with its own Proper Readings.
  • Dec. 20 is the Fourth Sunday of Advent, with its own Proper Readings, rather than those listed here, which would be used if Dec. 20 were a weekday.
  • Several "Memorials" of saints occur during Advent; on these days, the readings for the corresponding Advent Weekday should normally still be used:
    Dec. 7 - St. Ambrose; Dec. 13 - St. Lucy; Dec. 14 - St. John of the Cross.
  • Several "Optional Memorials" of saints also occur during Advent; but again, the readings for the corresponding Advent Weekday should normally still be used on these days:
    Dec. 4 - St. John of Damascus; Dec. 6 - St. Nicholas; Dec. 9 - St. Juan Diego; Dec. 11 - St. Damasus I; Dec. 12 - St. Jane Frances de Chantal; Dec. 21 - St. Peter Canisius; Dec. 23 - St. John of Kanty.

Rationale: The "General Introduction to the Lectionary" (Second Edition, 1981) briefly explains the rationale for the reading choices for the Weekdays of Advent:

"There are two series of readings: one to be used from the beginning of Advent until 16 December; the other from 17 to 24 December.
In the first part of Advent there are readings from the Book of Isaiah, distributed in accord with the sequence of the book itself and including the more important texts that are also read on the Sundays. For the choice of the weekday Gospel the first reading has been taken into consideration. On Thursday of the second week the readings from the Gospel concerning John the Baptist begin. The first reading is either a continuation of Isaiah or a text chosen in view of the Gospel.
In the last week before Christmas the events that immediately prepared for the Lord's birth are presented from the Gospel of Matthew (chapter 1) and Luke (chapter 1). The texts in the first reading, chosen in view of the Gospel reading, are from different Old Testament books and include important Messianic prophecies." (Lectionary for Mass, "Introduction," chap. 5, par. 94.)

Three Parts of Advent: As the above Rationale shows, Advent can actually be subdivided into three parts (not just two), based on different emphases in the readings:
the first focus is on the prophet Isaiah, the second on the "precursor" John the Baptist, and the third on the events just before the birth of Jesus:

  • Part ONE: Focus on Readings from Isaiah (with complementary Gospel texts)
    • 1st Week, Monday (Years B&C): Isa 2:1-5 – "The Lord will gather all nations into the eternal peace of the Kingdom of God."
      1st Week, Monday (Year A): Isa 4:2-6 – "There will be splendor for the survivors."
      cf. Matt 8:5-11 – "Many will come from the east and the west into the Kingdom of heaven."
       
    • 1st Week, Tuesday: Isa 11:1-10 – "The Spirit of the LORD God shall rest upon him."
      cf. Luke 10:21-24 – "Jesus rejoices in the Holy Spirit."
       
    • 1st Week, Wednesday: Isa 25:6-10a – "The Lord invites us to his feast and will wipe away the tears from all faces."
      cf. Matt 15:29-37 – "Jesus heals many and multiplies the bread."
       
    • 1st Week, Thursday: Isa 26:1-6 – "Let in a nation that is just, one that keeps faith."
      cf. Matt 7:21, 24-27 – "Whoever does the will of my Father will enter the Kingdom of heaven."
       
    • 1st Week, Friday: Isa 29:17-24 – "On that day, the eyes of the blind shall see."
      cf. Matt 9:27-31 – "Believing in Jesus, two who were blind are cured."
       
    • 1st Week, Saturday: Isa 30:19-21, 23-26 – "The Merciful One will show you mercy when you cry out."
      cf. Matt 9:35—10:1, 5a, 6-8 – "At the sight of the crowds, Jesus' heart was moved with pity for them."
       
    • 2nd Week, Monday: Isa 35:1-10 – "God himself will come and save you."
      cf. Luke 5:17-26 – "We have seen incredible things today."
       
    • 2nd Week, Tuesday: Isa 40:1-11 – "God consoles his people."
      cf. Matt 18:12-14 – "God does not will that the little ones be lost."
       
    • 2nd Week, Wednesday: Isa 40:25-31 – "The LORD God is almighty and gives strength to the fainting."
      cf. Matt 11:28-30 – "Come to me, all you who labor."
       
  • Part TWO: Focus on Readings about John the Baptist
    • 2nd Week, Thursday: Matt 11:11-15 – "None greater than John the Baptist has been born."
    • 2nd Week, Friday: Matt 11:16-19 – "They listened neither to John nor to the Son of Man."
    • 2nd Week, Saturday: Matt 17:9a, 10-13 – "Elijah has already come, and they did not recognize him."
    • 3rd Week, Monday: Matt 21:23-27 – "John's baptism: where did it come from?"
    • 3rd Week, Tuesday: Matt 21:28-32 – "John came and sinners believed in him."
    • 3rd Week, Wednesday: Luke 7:18b-23 – "Go back and tell John what you have seen and heard."
    • 3rd Week, Thursday: Luke 7:24-30 – "John is the messenger who prepares the way of the Lord."
    • 3rd Week, Friday: John 5:33-36 – "John was a burning and shining lamp."
       
  • Part THREE: Focus on the Events that immediately prepared for the Lord's birth
    • Dec. 17: Matt 1:1-17 – The genealogy of Jesus Christ, the Son of David.
    • Dec. 18: Matt 1:18-24 – The dreams of Joseph about the conception and birth of Jesus
    • Dec. 19: Luke 1:5-25 – The birth of John the Baptist is announced by the angel Gabriel to Zechariah
    • Dec. 20: Luke 1:26-38 – The birth of Jesus is announced by the angel Gabriel to Mary
    • Dec. 21: Luke 1:39-45 – Mary visits Elizabeth, who calls Mary blessed
    • Dec. 22: Luke 1:46-56 – The Magnificat: Mary's song of praise to God
    • Dec. 23: Luke 1:57-66 – The birth, circumcision, and naming of John the Baptist
    • Dec. 24 (morning Mass): Luke 1:67-79 – The Benedictus: Zechariah's hymn blessing God

The Season of Christmas:

In the Lectionary for Mass, the readings for December 26-28 are listed in the "Proper of the Saints" rather than among the weekdays of Christmas; but they are included here to provide a comprehensive overview of the readings for the Christmas Season, esp. its use of the complete text of 1 John in the First Readings.

The readings for the Solemnities and Major Feasts during the Christmas Season are listed in the Sunday Lectionary: Holy Family (Sunday after Christmas; or Dec. 30 if Christmas itself is a Sunday); Mary, Mother of God (Jan. 1); Epiphany of the Lord (Jan. 6, or the Sunday after Jan. 1), and Baptism of the Lord (the Sunday after Epiphany; or the Monday after Epiphany if Epiphany is Jan. 7 or 8). Each of these Solemnities/Feasts has both a First Reading and a Second Reading before the Gospel, as listed on the webpage for Christmas Sundays & Feasts.

Several "Memorials" and "Optional Memorials" of saints also occur during Christmas season; but the readings for the corresponding Christmas Weekday should normally still be used on these days:
Dec. 29 - St. Thomas Becket; Dec. 31 - St. Sylvester I; Jan. 2 - Sts. Basil the Great and Gregory Nazianzen; Jan. 3 - Holy Name of Jesus; Jan. 4 - St. Elizabeth Ann Seton; Jan. 5 - St. John Neumann; Jan. 6 - Bl. André Bessette; Jan. 7 - St. Raymond of Peñafort.

Rationale: The "General Introduction to the Lectionary" (Second Edition, 1981) briefly explains the rationale for the reading choices for the Weekdays of the Christmas Season:

"From 29 December on, there is a continuous reading of the whole of the First Letter of John, which actually begins earlier, on 27 December, the Feast of St. John the Evangelist, and on 28 December, the Feast of the Holy Innocents. The Gospels relate manifestations of the Lord: events of Jesus' childhood from the Gospel of Luke (29-30 December); passages from the first chapter of the Gospel of John (31 December - 5 January); other manifestations of the Lord from the four Gospels (7-12 January)." (Lectionary for Mass, "Introduction," chap. 5, par. 96.)
